# Human Decision Guide: Advocacy Programme Decisions

## Your Role

The Advocacy Tracker identifies customers who are candidates for advocacy activities -- case studies, references, testimonials, speaking opportunities, beta programmes, and advisory boards. Your job is to decide who to approach, when to ask, and how to manage the relationship so advocacy feels like recognition, not extraction. The agent surfaces candidates. You cultivate advocates.

## When You Will See This

Advocacy candidate alerts are generated:

1. **Health score trigger** -- when bi-health-score identifies a promoter-level NPS response or sustained health score above 85 for 90+ days
2. **Value evidence trigger** -- when pa-value-reporter generates strong, quantifiable ROI evidence that could become case study material
3. **Sales or marketing request** -- when your Go-to-Market team needs a customer reference for a specific use case, segment, or deal
4. **Monthly** -- advocacy pipeline review and advocate health check to ensure active advocates are still engaged and willing

## Decision Framework

### 1. Assess readiness

Not every happy customer is an advocacy candidate. Before approaching, check:
- Have they achieved a measurable outcome they would be willing to share publicly? Vague satisfaction is not enough. You need numbers, milestones, or transformations.
- Is the champion secure enough in their role to publicly endorse your product? New hires, people in political turmoil, or those facing budget scrutiny may not want the visibility.
- Is the company's brand association positive for yours? Brand alignment matters for case studies and co-marketing.
- Is the timing right? Do not ask during a renewal negotiation, an open escalation, a leadership transition, or immediately after resolving a major issue.

### 2. Choose the ask

| Advocacy Type | Effort for Customer | Value for You | Best Candidates |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Internal reference** | Low (one call) | High (closes deals) | Any satisfied customer with measurable outcomes |
| **Written testimonial** | Low (approve a quote) | Medium | Customers with strong quotes already captured from QBRs or emails |
| **Case study** | Medium (interview + review) | Very high | Customers with compelling before/after transformation stories |
| **Speaking engagement** | High (time + preparation) | Very high (credibility) | Senior leaders who enjoy public visibility and have a genuine story to tell |
| **Advisory board** | High (ongoing commitment) | High (strategic input) | Strategic accounts with engaged executives who want to shape your roadmap |
| **Beta programme** | Variable | High (product feedback) | Power users who give constructive, specific feedback -- not just "looks great" |

### 3. Sequence the relationship

Start with the lowest-effort ask. Build the advocacy relationship gradually. A customer who agrees to be an internal reference today is a testimonial candidate in three months, a case study candidate in six months, and a conference speaker in a year. Rushing the progression burns goodwill.

## Special Handling

### Advocacy fatigue

Track how often you ask each advocate for something. More than twice per quarter is too much for most contacts. Rotate your advocates and invest in building new ones rather than over-using your best ones. The fastest way to lose an advocate is to make them feel like a marketing asset instead of a valued partner.

### Quid pro quo

The best advocacy relationships are reciprocal. Offer something in return: early access to features, executive access at your company, speaking opportunities at your events, genuine public recognition, or simply a handwritten thank-you note. Advocacy that flows only in one direction eventually stops flowing. Make it a partnership.

### The reluctant advocate

Some customers love your product but will not do public advocacy -- company policy, personal preference, or industry norms (regulated industries often restrict public endorsements). Respect this completely. Internal references (anonymous or named) and private advisory participation are still valuable. Never pressure a reluctant advocate -- you risk the relationship for a marketing asset.
