# Human Decision Guide: Benchmark Data Usage

## Your Role

The Benchmark Engine computes how an account compares to segment peers across adoption, usage, and engagement metrics. Your job is to decide which benchmarks to share, how to frame them, and whether the comparison is motivating or counterproductive for this specific customer. The agent computes the math. You manage the message.

## When You Will See This

Benchmark data is generated:

1. **QBR preparation** -- benchmark slides for cc-qbr-deck-builder
2. **Adoption conversation** -- peer comparison context for gap discussions
3. **On-demand** -- you requested competitive positioning data for a customer conversation
4. **Monthly refresh** -- updated benchmark positions across all accounts

## Decision Framework

### 1. Validate the cohort

Before sharing any benchmark:
- Is the comparison cohort appropriate? A 50-person company being compared to 500-person companies produces misleading data
- Is the cohort size sufficient? Benchmarks from a cohort of 12 are noisy. From 50+, they are reliable
- Does the customer see themselves in the cohort? Some customers will reject the comparison if they do not identify with the peer group

### 2. Decide on action

| Situation | When to Use | Next Step |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Share as motivation** | Customer is competitive and responds well to peer positioning | Frame as: "Your peers achieve X -- here is how you can get there too." Use for underperforming metrics where action is clear |
| **Share as validation** | Customer is performing well relative to peers | Frame as: "Your team is in the top quartile." This builds confidence and reinforces the value of their investment |
| **Hold back** | Customer is significantly below peers and already frustrated | Sharing "you are below average" to a customer in crisis adds insult to injury. Address the underlying issues first |
| **Reframe the cohort** | Default cohort does not fit the customer's self-image | Adjust the comparison: "Compared to companies in your industry" vs. "compared to companies your size." The right framing makes the benchmark actionable |
| **Use internally only** | Benchmark reveals something the customer should not see (e.g., they are dramatically underperforming) | Use the insight for your strategy but do not present it to the customer until you have a plan to address the gap |

## Special Handling

### Below-Median Customers

Never use the words "below average," "underperforming," or "bottom quartile" with a customer. Frame as opportunity: "Similar companies typically achieve X on this metric -- there is room to drive more value here." Same data, different impact.

### Top Quartile Customers

High performers should hear it. This is earned recognition and it reinforces the product's value. Use it to strengthen the renewal conversation and plant expansion seeds: "You have maximised what your current tier offers -- the next tier unlocks [capability] that your peers at this level typically adopt."

### Benchmark Limitations

Benchmarks describe the cohort's current state, not what is possible. If the entire segment underutilises a feature, the median is low. An account at the median may still have massive untapped potential. Use benchmarks as context, not as a ceiling.
