# Human Decision Guide: Competitive Response Strategy

## Your Role

The Competitive Response Prep skill assembles the retention case when a competitive threat is detected — value evidence, switching cost analysis, differentiation, and a response framework by evaluation stage and trigger. Your job is to decide how to respond: whether to engage directly, how to frame the conversation, when to involve leadership, and what concessions (if any) are warranted. The agent arms you. You fight the fight.

## When You Will See This

Competitive response materials are generated when:

1. **bi-competitive-intel flags a signal** and you acknowledge it requires preparation
2. **You request materials** for a competitive conversation you know is coming
3. **lo-renewal-manager** flags a renewal as At Risk or Critical with a competitive signal

## Decision Framework

### 1. Assess the threat before reacting

Not every competitive signal warrants a defensive response. Before engaging:
- Is this a serious evaluation or a negotiating tactic? Customers sometimes mention competitors to get a better price
- What is the actual trigger? Addressing the root cause (product gap, service failure, pricing concern) is more effective than defending against the competitor
- What stage is the evaluation? Early consideration, active evaluation, and imminent decision require completely different approaches

### 2. Decide on approach

| Situation | When to Use | Next Step |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Proactive value reinforcement** | Early consideration — customer is just curious, not committed | Do not mention the competitor. Reinforce value delivered and deepen the relationship. Make the competitor's pitch irrelevant |
| **Root cause conversation** | Active evaluation triggered by a real gap (product, service, price) | Address the underlying issue directly: "Help me understand what prompted this." Solve the problem; do not sell against the competitor |
| **Direct engagement** | Decision is imminent and the customer is open to a final conversation | Ask for the chance to respond. Be honest about where you are stronger and where the competitor may be better. Credibility matters more than spin |
| **Escalate to leadership** | Strategic account, C-suite-level evaluation, or executive relationship required | Involve your manager and consider VP-to-VP or CEO engagement. Match the level of the conversation |
| **Accept the loss gracefully** | Customer has decided and the relationship is more valuable than a last-ditch effort | Do not fight a decided customer. Maintain the relationship. Churned customers return when the competitor disappoints |

## Special Handling

### Do Not Lead with the Competitor's Name

Naming the competitor validates the comparison. If you have not confirmed who they are evaluating, do not speculate. Focus on your value and address the customer's stated concerns. If they name the competitor, you can address it directly.

### Acknowledge Real Gaps Honestly

If the competitor is stronger in the area the customer cares about, acknowledge it. Then redirect to where you are stronger and what switching would cost them. A CSM who pretends every competitive disadvantage does not exist loses credibility instantly.

### Service Failure as a Trigger

If the evaluation was triggered by a service failure on your side (poor support, broken feature, missed commitment), the competitor did not win — you lost. Own the failure explicitly. "I understand why you are looking at alternatives after what happened with [issue]" is the right opening. Then show what has changed.
