# Human Decision Guide: Stakeholder Map Review

## Your Role

The Stakeholder Mapper builds and maintains a structured view of key contacts -- their roles, influence levels, disposition toward your product, relationship depth, and coverage status. Your job is to validate the map against your relationship knowledge, identify the gaps that create risk, and decide where to invest your limited multi-threading effort. The agent maps the data. You map the politics.

## When You Will See This

Stakeholder map outputs are generated:

1. **Continuously** -- as CRM contacts, activities, and engagement signals change
2. **Weekly** -- coverage gap analysis across all accounts in your portfolio
3. **On request** -- when you or your manager requests a stakeholder map for a specific account (common before QBRs, renewals, or escalations)

## Decision Framework

### 1. Validate the map

The agent builds the map from CRM data, meeting attendance, email patterns, and support interactions. You validate from relationship knowledge that data cannot capture:
- Is the influence classification correct? Some contacts have informal influence that never appears in tracked channels. The person who advises the CFO over coffee matters more than their title suggests.
- Are there stakeholders the system doesn't see? Board members, external advisors, internal champions who interact through untracked channels, or influential end users who don't appear in CRM.
- Is anyone's disposition misclassified? A contact marked as "champion" who is actually neutral-to-skeptical is a dangerous blind spot. A contact marked as "detractor" who is actually persuadable is a missed opportunity.

### 2. Identify coverage gaps

| Gap Type | Risk | Priority |
|----------|------|----------|
| **No executive sponsor relationship** | Critical -- no air cover for renewal, escalation, or strategic conversation | Immediate. Every strategic account needs an executive relationship |
| **Single-threaded to one champion** | High -- any change to that person endangers the entire account | High. Begin multi-threading this week |
| **No relationship with economic buyer** | High -- renewal and expansion decisions happen without your input | High. Request introduction through your champion |
| **Missing department coverage** | Medium -- adoption limited to one team, expansion blocked | Medium. Identify the entry point into the next department |
| **No end-user relationships** | Low-Medium -- product feedback loop broken, usage risks invisible | Lower. But consider a user advisory group or feedback session |

### 3. Plan multi-threading actions

For each gap, define a specific action with a specific timeline:
- Who will you ask for the introduction? Your existing champion is usually the bridge.
- What is your reason for meeting the new contact? Never "just wanted to connect." Always value-led: "I'd like to understand how [their team] is using the platform and whether there are opportunities we're missing."
- What is the timeline? Multi-threading is urgent for single-threaded accounts and important-but-not-urgent for well-covered accounts.

## Special Handling

### Political complexity

Some orgs have competing internal factions -- product teams vs. operations, new leadership vs. inherited teams, HQ vs. regional offices. Your stakeholder map should note alliances and tensions. Not to exploit them, but to avoid inadvertently aligning with one side in an internal dispute. Neutrality protects the account relationship. If you must choose a side, choose the one with budget authority and strategic alignment to your value proposition.

### Mapping without CRM data

For accounts where CRM data is sparse (new accounts, acquired accounts, or accounts with poor data hygiene), build the map through conversation. Ask your primary contact: "Who else should we be keeping informed?" and "Who would need to approve a renewal or expansion?" These questions are natural, non-threatening, and fill your map faster than any data integration.

### The disappearing stakeholder

When a contact on your map goes completely silent -- no meetings, no emails, no support interactions -- this is either a departure signal or a disengagement signal. Both require investigation. Check with your other contacts before reaching out directly. "I noticed we haven't connected with [name] recently -- is everything OK on their end?" is a natural and non-alarming way to surface the information you need.
