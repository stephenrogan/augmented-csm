# Human Decision Guide: Engagement Pattern Interpretation

## Your Role

The Engagement Tracker monitors interaction frequency, depth, and breadth across your accounts -- meetings attended, emails exchanged, support contacts, product usage sessions, executive touchpoints. Your job is to interpret the patterns, decide what the engagement trajectory means for the relationship, and distinguish between data noise and genuine signals. The agent measures frequency. You assess meaning.

## When You Will See This

Engagement alerts surface when:

1. **No-reply streak** -- 3+ outbound emails to the same contact without a response
2. **Champion cooling** -- your most important contact's engagement frequency is declining against their own baseline
3. **Complete silence** -- no touchpoints of any kind on an account for 30+ days
4. **Cadence compliance gap** -- you are behind on expected touchpoints for accounts in your portfolio
5. **Weekly engagement summary** -- portfolio-wide engagement health with movers, alerts, and trend data

## Decision Framework

### 1. Interpret the signal before acting

Engagement decline has multiple causes. Diagnose before responding:
- Is this seasonal? Some contacts go quiet in Q4 budget season, summer holidays, or fiscal year-end. Compare to their own historical pattern, not a universal benchmark.
- Did something happen in the relationship? A difficult conversation, a missed commitment, or an unresolved support issue can explain silence without indicating departure intent.
- Is the contact just a passive communicator? Some relationships are healthy but quiet -- the customer responds when needed but does not initiate. Check product usage and health score alongside engagement data before concluding there is a problem.

### 2. Classify the pattern

| Pattern | What It Usually Means | Your Action |
|---------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Steady and broad** | Healthy multi-threaded relationship with regular touchpoints | Maintain cadence. Look for expansion opportunities. Do not over-engineer a working relationship |
| **Steady but narrowing** | Dependence on a single contact -- champion risk building | Actively multi-thread. Request introductions to other stakeholders. Every narrowing relationship is one departure away from crisis |
| **Declining across contacts** | Systemic disengagement -- potential churn signal | Investigate the cause. Plan re-engagement. Consider an executive touchpoint to reset the relationship |
| **Increasing sharply** | Could be positive (expansion interest, new initiative) or negative (escalation, frustration, evaluation) | Determine the driver before celebrating or panicking. Increased engagement is not inherently good |
| **Sporadic** | No established rhythm, customer likely sees the relationship as low-priority | Propose a regular cadence tied to specific value milestones. Give them a reason to show up consistently |

### 3. Decide what you know vs. what you are guessing

The tracker shows you behaviour. It cannot tell you intent. Before acting on an engagement trend, ask: do I have direct evidence of why this is changing, or am I inferring? If inferring, your first action is to validate -- not to intervene. Intervening on a wrong assumption can cause the problem you were trying to prevent.

## Special Handling

### Executive engagement flatline

When executive sponsors stop attending QBRs or responding to outreach, this is the single most dangerous engagement signal. It typically precedes churn by 60-90 days. Do not wait for more data. Escalate to your leadership for an executive-to-executive touchpoint before the gap widens further. Executive disengagement does not recover on its own.

### New contacts appearing

New stakeholders engaging with your product or team can signal expansion (new department adopting, new use case emerging) or transition risk (replacement evaluation, competitive assessment). Identify who they are and what their role is before interpreting the signal. A new VP of Operations appearing in usage data is very different from a new procurement analyst appearing in support tickets.

### Frequency is not quality

A CSM with perfect engagement scores who never moves the relationship forward is not succeeding. A CSM with lower-than-expected touchpoint frequency who has deep, strategic conversations with key stakeholders may be building a stronger account. Use engagement data as a trigger for investigation, not as the final verdict on relationship health.

### Inbound ratio as a trust signal

When a customer stops initiating contact -- questions, requests, ideas, feedback -- it often means they have stopped seeing you as a resource. High inbound ratio (customer reaches out to you) means the customer sees value in the relationship. Low inbound ratio (you always initiate) means they have learned not to bother. This metric is worth a direct conversation.
