# Human Decision Guide: Cross-Functional Meeting Preparation

## Your Role

The Cross-Functional Prep skill assembles context, talking points, and data for meetings with internal teams -- product, engineering, sales, support, leadership. Your job is to decide what narrative to lead with, what to ask for, and how to position CS priorities against competing internal agendas. The agent provides comprehensive materials. You decide what makes the cut.

## When You Will See This

Prep materials are generated:

1. **24 hours before a recurring internal meeting** detected via calendar integration (manager 1:1, pipeline review, cross-functional sync)
2. **On request** when you are preparing for a specific meeting
3. **Weekly** for standing meetings with configured prep schedules

## Decision Framework

### 1. Define your objective

Before reviewing the prep material, answer: what is the one thing I need from this meeting? Everything else is context. If you cannot state your objective in one sentence, you are not ready for the meeting.

### 2. Assess the audience

| Audience | They Care About | Lead With |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Product** | Adoption data, feature requests backed by revenue impact, competitive gaps | Usage evidence + revenue at risk if gap persists |
| **Engineering** | Reproducible issues, technical specifics, priority justification | Ticket data, frequency, customer impact quantified |
| **Sales** | Expansion pipeline, reference customers, competitive positioning | Account health + expansion readiness signals |
| **Support** | Case patterns, escalation trends, self-service gaps | Ticket volume trends + customer sentiment data |
| **Leadership** | NRR, cost to serve, strategic risk, logo retention | Portfolio-level metrics + specific accounts that need executive attention |

### 3. Filter the prep material

The agent will over-prepare by design -- it is cheaper to cut than to miss something. Your job is to cut ruthlessly:
- Remove anything that doesn't support your one objective
- Remove data points that require heavy explanation -- if it needs a footnote in a meeting, it weakens the argument
- Keep it to three supporting points maximum. Three is persuasive. Five is a lecture.

### 4. Decide your ask

| Decision | When to Use | Next Step |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Request specific action** | You need something concrete from the audience (feature prioritisation, resource allocation, escalation support) | State the ask clearly, support it with the three strongest data points, propose a timeline |
| **Share information** | The meeting is for alignment, not action | Be concise. Provide the update. Do not pad informational updates with asks that dilute your credibility for when you need something real |
| **Seek input** | You need guidance on a decision (account strategy, escalation approach, pricing response) | Frame the decision clearly, present your options with trade-offs, ask for a recommendation |

## Special Handling

### Escalation meetings

When the cross-functional meeting is an escalation (customer at risk, P1 issue, contract dispute), lead with impact and timeline, not backstory. The audience needs to know: what's at stake, what's needed, and by when. Backstory is available if asked -- do not lead with it. Urgency creates focus. History creates debate.

### Feature request prioritisation

Never present a single customer's feature request as a priority. Aggregate across accounts: "12 accounts representing $2.4M ARR have requested this capability." Volume and revenue weight earn prioritisation. Single-account asks earn a backlog ticket. The exception: strategic accounts where the relationship itself justifies priority treatment -- but frame it as strategic risk, not as a favour.

### Recurring meetings that have lost purpose

If you are receiving prep materials for a meeting that no longer delivers value, that is a signal. Either redefine the meeting's purpose or propose cancelling it. Meeting inertia is one of the most expensive forms of organisational waste.
